Response: Hypertext of Claire Evans’ BroadBand




When I think of the internet today, it’s hard for me to break out of that link to link connection we’re all so used
to. It’s another conversation, but it’s definitely a dangerous pattern to get stuck in, as link to link, as explained
by the chapter, isn’t the only way to think about accessing information on the World Wide Web.

When Evans was explaining Wendy Hall’s logic behind Microcosm, it was quite hard for me to follow because I had to
re-define my definition of link. The idea that a link could matter more than the actual content on separate pages is
so foreign to me because the way I’ve always used the internet was dependent on this “search and find” method. It
makes me wonder what the internet might look like if we had adopted Microcosm’s interface. If the internet of today
is a “supernova,” might the Microcosm net look more like separate solar systems? It makes me a little disappointed
that links have been thrown to the wayside. Links could be so helpful in organizing a now massive web that’s hard
to make sense of. They could also create pertinent algorithms for individual users, which our current net doesn’t
care about. But like Cathy Marshall mentioned, the users are the program. To me, this statement just points to the
fact that although a program is meant to do “x,” people are always going to find a way to transform it into
something outside of the developer’s original perception of a particular program’s allowance.

Perhaps this points to a future on the web where links become more useful than they currently are. It’s not too hard
to imagine that when the internet becomes too commercialized and overrun with so many different voices, then
personalized experiences could begin to take hold to give users a feeling of individualism on the web again.



Questions:

  1. Is there a way to give power back to the users regarding online programs? The users are the program, so isn’t it
    inherent that they should have some role in designing programs distributed to the public?

  2. How does a link in essence become useful? It’s hard to imagine given the only power its granted currently is
    connecting to larger, more “important” pages. But given more attention, could links become a net unto themselves?
