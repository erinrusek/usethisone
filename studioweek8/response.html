March 8th, 2020

Response: Ursula Leguin, ‘A Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction’,
Ted Chiang’s 'The Tower of Babylon’
and Jorge Luis Borges’ ’Library of Babel’




Each of these readings managed to get me locked in existential/anxious thought on a
Sunday afternoon, especially Ted Chiang’s ‘The Tower of Babylon’. What Chiang’s
and Borges’ stories have in common is the narrative that Leguin explains in ‘A
Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction’. The stories Chiang and Borges tell are quite
similar, one describing the ascent towards the vault of heaven, and the discovery
that there is no way to break inside of heaven without looping back to Earth. The
other story describes the universe as a library full of books which, in the
pursuit of the meaning of it all (in the form of one book), had driven people from
hopeful, to despair and chaos. Interestingly enough, I believe Leguin’s
explanation of this “heroic” storytelling which omits the “unnecessary” fragments
that help the hero through their journey could be a metaphor for the trajectory in
which the web has grown and its limitations that have been overlooked in pursuit
of a greater more advanced internet.
Many of the problems we have today regarding the internet revolve around subjects
such as corporate takeovers, fear of A.I. and the want for more freedom and across
this digital universe amongst others. Perhaps in a somewhat dark sense, like
Leguin’s fiction, the issues surrounding the internet today will never really go
away. They may be disguised as other issues with different heroes, but we will
always be facing the same issues, in an attempt to gain the perfect internet (such
as the search for what’s ). Like the stories, the history of the internet is one
big loop. Maybe however, on a lighter note, understanding this could put a bigger
emphasis on archiving the present like the Wayback machine, living in the now on
the internet and continuing to find an equilibrium no matter how the issues may
transform in the future. An easier way to say this might simply be to keep
reminding ourselves of the internet’s history, and learn how to best handle progress
from the positive endeavors of the past.


Questions:
  1. Will there ever be a perfect internet where everyone’s wants/needs are satisfied?
  2. How do we properly give credit to those which helped the “heroes” of our present in regards
    to freeing up the web? Is it possible to do so?
